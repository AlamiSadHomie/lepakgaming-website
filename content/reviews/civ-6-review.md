## The Game That Made Me a Strategy Guy

Let me be upfront — I've always had a soft spot for strategy games. There's something deeply satisfying about planning several moves ahead, watching your decisions unfold over time, and outsmarting opponents through pure brain power instead of reflexes. Whether it's tower defense, real-time strategy, or even chess, I'm drawn to games that make you think.

So when I finally sat down with Sid Meier's Civilization VI, I thought I knew what I was getting into.

I had absolutely no idea.

## My Entry Point Into the Series

Here's the thing — Civilization VI isn't just a great game for me. It's *the* game that introduced me to the entire Civilization franchise. I didn't start with Civ V or IV like a lot of veterans did. VI was my first, and honestly? I couldn't be happier about that.

From the very first session, I was hooked. The concept alone is brilliant — you start as a tiny little civilization in 4000 BC with nothing but a settler and a warrior, and from there you're building cities, researching technologies, forging alliances, waging wars, and trying to guide your people all the way to a space age victory. The scope of it is genuinely breathtaking when you think about it.

What pulled me in immediately was how everything connects. Every decision matters. Settle your city in the wrong spot and you'll regret it 200 turns later. Pick the wrong technology to research and suddenly you're behind everyone else in a critical era. It's the kind of game that rewards thinking ahead, and for someone who enjoys that type of gameplay, it's basically perfect.

## The "One More Turn" Trap is Real

I'm not going to pretend I'm good at this game. Because I'm not. At least not yet.

Right now I'm grinding away at Emperor difficulty, and it is genuinely humbling. Emperor is the point where the AI stops feeling like a pushover and starts actually playing the game — expanding aggressively, building wonders before you can, and generally making your life difficult in every way possible.

My current goal is simple: win at Emperor. Just once. Just to prove to myself that I can. After that, I want to climb — Immortal, then Deity, which is apparently where the real madness lives.

The thing is, every failed run teaches me something new. Maybe I settled too close to another civ and triggered a war I wasn't ready for. Maybe I ignored a wonder and let someone else grab it. Maybe I spread myself too thin trying to build too many cities at once. Each loss stings, but it also makes you smarter about the next attempt.

That's what great strategy games do. They make losing feel like progress.

## The Depth Is Insane

What makes Civilization VI genuinely special is how much there is to learn and master. Victory conditions alone give you so many different ways to play — domination, science, culture, religion, diplomacy, score. Each one requires a completely different strategy and mindset.

The new district system introduced in Civ VI adds a whole layer of city planning that I love. You're not just plopping buildings down anymore — you're deciding where to physically place districts on the map, and the terrain around them gives you adjacency bonuses. It turns city building into a puzzle, and I'm a sucker for that kind of depth.

Different leaders and civilizations also completely change how you approach the game. Each one has unique abilities, units, and bonuses that push you toward a particular playstyle. Some runs I'll go wide and conquer everything in sight. Other runs I'll turtle up, build tall, and chase a science victory. The variety keeps it fresh even after dozens of hours.

## One Final Word: About That AI Science Victory

Okay. I need to vent about something.

Why is it that no matter what I do, what strategy I run, or how well I think my game is going — there's always some AI civilization quietly building up to a science victory in the background? I'll be focused on expanding, managing wars, keeping my people happy... and then suddenly I get a notification that some random civ just launched a satellite and is about to win the game.

Every. Single. Time.

It doesn't matter if I'm playing as a domination civ. It doesn't matter if I think I'm ahead. Somewhere out there, an AI is building campuses, researching technologies, and plotting to win by sending a rocket to space before I've even figured out my own victory path.

At this point I think Civilization VI's AI has one true goal in life: deny Nafiz from winning. And honestly? Respect. It's working.

## The Bottom Line

**Rating: 5/5 Stars — Absolutely Legendary**

Civilization VI is the kind of game that ruins your sleep schedule and makes you cancel plans. It's endlessly deep, wildly replayable, and genuinely rewards the time you put into learning it. As my first entry into the Civilization series, it's made me a lifelong fan — and a forever student of the game.

I still haven't beaten Emperor. But I will. Eventually.

And when I do, I'm going straight to Immortal.

### The Exceptional
- Massive depth with countless ways to win
- Incredibly replayable with different civs and leaders
- District system adds smart city-planning decisions
- Satisfying long-term strategic thinking
- Enormous amount of content (especially with DLC)

### The Honest Frustrations
- AI will humble you constantly (especially at higher difficulties)
- Learning curve can be steep for newcomers
- "One more turn" syndrome will destroy your sleep

### Who Should Play This?

If you enjoy thinking, planning, and building things over a long period of time — this is your game. Strategy fans will feel right at home, but even if you're new to the genre, Civ VI is an incredible entry point. Just don't blame me when you look up and three hours have passed.

Now if you'll excuse me, I need to reload my Emperor save and stop that AI from winning by science victory. Again.

**Final Score: 5/5 — One more turn. Always one more turn.**
